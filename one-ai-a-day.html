The framing

Most AI learning asks too much up front — long articles, full courses, weekly newsletters. I wanted the opposite: one idea a day, five seconds of attention, no obligation to return.

Can AI literacy feel like checking a calendar, not taking a course?

The product is a calendar the reader explores. Click any date to open one concept. Use the arrows to browse months. Share a specific date with anyone. That is the whole interaction model.

How it works

The reader controls the pace

Nothing is pushed. No email, no notification, no streak counter. The calendar sits still until the reader taps a date — then one idea opens in place. Close it, open another, or leave. The lack of nagging is the point.

Two tracks, one calendar

The 1st of every month is always a Beginner's Journey step — twelve foundational milestones across the year. Every other date is a deeper concept card. Same calendar, two reading paths, no toggle needed.

Deep links as a feature, not an afterthought

Every date has its own URL. ?day=14 opens the 14th of whatever month is showing; ?day=1&month=6 opens June's milestone. Sharing a single idea takes one click.

Thematic parts

The year is divided into four thematic parts. Each contains 30 concept cards on a consistent schema — headline idea, short explanation, custom visual, and a "what it isn't" clarification where it helps.

The rotation means the same calendar serves a beginner in January and a practitioner in October. The surface stays stable; the depth shifts.

Design decisions

Two categories of decisions mattered most — what to build into the experience, and what to leave out.

Built in
Accessibility as default
WCAG AA contrast at actual rendered sizes. Respects prefers-reduced-motion. Keyboard navigable.
Built in
Seasonal context
Each month has its own hand-composed SVG scene above the grid, giving immediate orientation in the year.
Left out
No accounts or streaks
No login, no email capture, no "days in a row" pressure. Learning should not carry guilt.
Left out
No reactions or comments
Storage complexity for signal that was not needed. The share button does the social work.

Working with Claude Project

The site was built iteratively in a Claude Project across many sessions. I defined the audience, chose the structure, wrote and reviewed every concept, and made every trade-off call. Claude turned those decisions into working HTML, CSS, and SVG.

What worked

  • Rendered options before code. Asking for 2–3 visual previews up front made iterations cheaper than "build then revise."
  • Honest trade-offs on demand. When I asked what the downsides of a choice were, I got them.
  • Content stays mine. Every concept was reviewed for accuracy; a few had source errors that I fixed by hand.

AI collaboration is directed work, not delegated work. The output tracks the specificity of the direction.

Takeaways

1

Structure teaches. The Day 1 / Days 2–31 split and the four thematic parts do more onboarding than any tutorial would.

2

Subtraction is a PM skill. Every feature not built was a decision. No login, no streaks, no comments — each removal made the product sharper.

3

Interactivity does not need a backend. A thoughtful front-end can feel like an app. The calendar, deep links, and share flow all run client-side with no server.

4

Accessibility compounds. Designing for WCAG AA and reduced motion from day one costs less than retrofitting, and quietly improves the experience for everyone.
